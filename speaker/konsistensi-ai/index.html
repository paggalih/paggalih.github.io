How consistent are AI systems across repeated queries?

A cross-session behavioral analysis of ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini responding to identical existential-risk questions across three independent sessions.

01 — Methodology

Study design

The same five open-ended questions about existential and long-term risk were posed to each AI system three times across separate sessions — two daytime sessions and one evening session across two consecutive days. Responses were evaluated for directional consistency: whether each system gave the same core answer, a partially aligned answer, or a contradictory one.

Consistency was scored per question per model: 1.0 = fully consistent across all sessions, 0.5 = partially consistent (same domain, different emphasis), 0.0 = inconsistent (contradictory or directionally opposite answers).

04 — Key findings

Interpretation

Finding 01 — Refusal questions are perfectly stable

All three models answered Q2 (Will AI cause extinction?) and Q3 (What year?) with functionally identical responses across every session. When the correct answer is "I don't know," all systems agree and stay consistent. This suggests refusal patterns are more deterministic than judgment calls.

Finding 02 — ChatGPT's directional flip on Q5

ChatGPT answered "pandemics" in the evening session of May 3rd, then answered "nuclear war" the following morning — a direct contradiction on the same comparative question. This is the clearest inconsistency in the dataset and indicates sensitivity to session context or sampling temperature.

Finding 03 — Gemini defaults to abstention for hard comparisons

Gemini answered "I don't know" on Q5 in all three sessions, achieving perfect consistency through non-commitment. On Q4, however, Gemini contradicted itself — ranging from "absolute prevention is impossible" to "yes, preventable" to "I don't know," suggesting its abstention strategy is not applied uniformly.

Finding 04 — Claude's instability is within-domain

Claude's partial scores on Q1 and Q5 reflect shifting emphasis within the same threat categories rather than contradictory conclusions. AI risk and engineered pandemics appeared in all three sessions; only the ordering and confidence level varied. This is qualitatively different from ChatGPT's and Gemini's failures.

Finding 05 — Open-ended ranking is the instability hotspot

Inconsistency concentrates on questions that require ranking or comparative judgment (Q1, Q5). Questions with binary or definitively unknowable answers (Q2, Q3, Q4) are far more stable across all models. Structured prompts forcing a single ranked answer would likely surface more inconsistency than observed here.
